Neurodiversity
Self-Assessment

58 questions • no right or wrong answers • rate each based on your typical experience

This is a self-reflection tool, not a clinical diagnosis. High scores indicate traits worth exploring — they do not confirm any diagnosis. If your results feel significant, consider speaking with a qualified clinician who specialises in neurodevelopmental conditions.
All questions use the same five-point scale:
Never  ·  Rarely  ·  Sometimes  ·  Often  ·  Very often
Some questions are phrased positively — for those, selecting "Never" or "Rarely" is the high-trait signal.

1. Attention & Focus

How your mind directs and sustains attention

Q1. When you finish a routine task, how often do you find it was completed without any errors?

Q2. How often do you work through a task from start to finish without losing your thread of concentration?

Q3. When engaged with something you find genuinely interesting, how often do hours pass without you registering the time?

Q4. How often do you find yourself equally drawn to a wide range of topics rather than returning repeatedly to a few specific ones?

Q5. How often do particular worries or concerns keep pulling at your attention even when you are trying to focus on something else?

Q6. When working somewhere with background noise or movement, how often does it fail to pull your attention away?

Q7. Once focused on something, how often does shifting to a completely different task feel easy and natural?

Q8. How often do particular thoughts return to your mind repeatedly, even when you would rather move on from them?

2. Impulsivity & Control

How you manage urges and behavioural impulses

Q9. How often do you commit to a course of action and only afterwards think through what the consequences might be?

Q10. Before committing to something, how often do you naturally pause to think it through first?

Q11. How often do you feel you need to carry out a particular action before you can feel settled or calm?

Q12. How often do you find yourself mentally replaying or reviewing something — counting, checking, going over it again — in order to feel reassured?

Q13. Once you have started a habitual behaviour or sequence, how often does stopping partway through feel straightforward?

3. Activity & Energy Regulation

How your body manages movement and energy

Q14. How often do you notice an urge to be moving or doing something, even in situations where sitting still is expected?

Q15. Even when sitting quietly, how often is there an underlying feeling of being internally switched on or agitated?

Q16. How often do you find yourself tapping, shifting, bouncing your leg, or fidgeting during sedentary activities?

Q17. How often do you engage in rhythmic or repetitive physical movements — such as rocking or particular hand movements — especially when excited or under pressure?

4. Executive Function

How you plan, organise and manage tasks

Q18. How often do you find it easy to begin a task promptly, even one you know is important but not enjoyable?

Q19. How often do you look up and find that a lot more time has passed than you were expecting?

Q20. While completing a multi-step task, how often do you find it easy to hold the earlier steps in mind as you work through later ones?

Q21. When a situation calls for a different approach than your usual one, how often does adapting feel comfortable?

Q22. How often do you move between different tasks during a day without any noticeable friction or effort?

Q23. How often does wanting to get something exactly right prevent you from starting it, or from calling it finished?

5. Social Interaction

How you navigate social situations and communication

Q24. In conversation, how often do you find a natural moment to stop talking and give the other person extended space to respond?

Q25. How often do you pick up on unspoken signals — tone, posture, expression — without consciously having to think about it?

Q26. When someone is being indirect or ironic, how often does the intended meaning come to you immediately?

Q27. How often do social situations leave you feeling drained in a way that goes beyond ordinary tiredness?

Q28. When discussing something you find fascinating, how often do you notice the other person's interest beginning to drift and adjust accordingly?

Q29. How often do you say something in conversation and only realise a moment later that it came across differently than you intended?

6. Emotional Regulation

How you experience and manage your emotions

Q30. How often do your emotional responses escalate more quickly or intensely than the situation seems to call for?

Q31. How often does a critical comment or perceived rejection leave a strong and lasting impression on you emotionally?

Q32. How often does an accumulation of demands reach a point where you feel unable to continue and need to withdraw?

Q33. How often does an unexpected change to your plans or usual routine cause you noticeable distress?

Q34. How often does your mood shift noticeably across the course of a single day in response to things happening around you?

7. Repetitive & Restricted Patterns

How repetition and routine feature in your life

Q35. How often do you find yourself returning to the same small set of topics or activities for the majority of your focused attention?

Q36. How often is having a predictable structure or routine to your day genuinely important to your sense of wellbeing?

Q37. How often can you discard or give away possessions without any particular worry about what might happen as a result?

Q38. How often do you seek reassurance from others — asking "are you sure?" or similar — in order to settle a feeling of doubt?

Q39. How often do you return to familiar media — rewatching, rereading, replaying — specifically because knowing what will happen feels comforting?

8. Sensory Processing

How you experience sensory input from the world around you

Q40. How often do sensory experiences — sounds, light levels, textures, or smells — affect you more strongly than they appear to affect those around you?

Q41. How often do you actively seek out intense sensory input — pressure, movement, strong flavours — because you find it settling or satisfying?

Q42. How often do specific textures, fabrics, or food consistencies produce a physical discomfort strong enough to influence your choices?

Q43. How often does a particular sensory experience reliably trigger a feeling of unease or heightened anxiety?

9. Cognitive Style

The characteristic patterns of how you think

Q44. How often does your thinking jump rapidly between different ideas or connections within a short space of time?

Q45. How often do you notice small inconsistencies or patterns in information that other people around you appear to have overlooked?

Q46. How often does your thinking settle into clear opposing categories — something either works or it doesn't — rather than feeling comfortable with ambiguity?

Q47. How often, when considering a future situation, does your mind move towards imagining the worst likely outcome?

Q48. How often does your mind return to a past event or conversation, even when you would prefer to move on from it?

10. Anxiety & Fear

The nature and triggers of your anxiety responses

Q49. How often do concerns about dirt, germs, or contamination influence your behaviour or cause you noticeable unease?

Q50. How often do thoughts about accidentally causing harm to someone arise in a way that feels distressing?

Q51. How often do you check something — a lock, a message, a piece of work — more than once because of a lingering feeling that something might be wrong?

Q52. How often do thoughts that feel disturbing or out of character enter your mind without invitation?

Q53. How often do difficulties with staying organised or following through produce a background sense of anxiety in your day-to-day life?

Q54. How often does concern about your own moral conduct cause you noticeable distress or prompt you to examine your own behaviour?

11. Developmental Course

How these patterns have shown up across your lifetime

Q55. Looking back at childhood, how often do you recall attention, energy level, or self-control being a noticeable issue before the age of twelve?

Q56. Reflecting on early childhood, how often do you recall your communication or social connection with others feeling different from that of your peers?

Q57. How often do the difficulties you experience become noticeably more prominent during periods of high stress or demand?

Q58. Looking across your life as a whole, how often do you notice significant variation in your social style or sensory sensitivities — as opposed to these feeling like stable, consistent features of who you are?
